\begin{abstract}
Photonic neural networks (PNNs) based on Mach--Zehnder interferometer (MZI) meshes
implement matrix--vector multiplication in the optical domain, but selecting among
proposed mesh topologies requires navigating tradeoffs between accuracy, hardware cost,
robustness, and physical loss mechanisms that have not been evaluated under identical
conditions.  We present a simulation-based Pareto benchmark of four topologies---Clements,
Reck, butterfly/FFT, and SCF fractal---evaluated with per-MZI insertion loss,
\emph{waveguide crossing loss}, and Monte Carlo phase-noise analysis (seven noise levels,
30 trials each) on four classification tasks (Vowel, MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10)
from $N{=}4$ to $N{=}128$ ports.  We observe that butterfly/FFT dominates the Pareto
front at all tested scales: at $N{=}64$ it outperforms Clements by $+3.3$ to $+4.8$
percentage points across all four datasets, with up to $2.8\times$ better noise robustness
and $100\times$ lower hardware cost.  Crossing loss, modeled at 0.02\,dB per waveguide
intersection, degrades butterfly accuracy by 0.7\,pp at $N{=}16$ and 3.2\,pp at $N{=}128$
(relative to zero crossing loss), but becomes far more damaging for SCF fractal at
large $N$ due to its $O(N^2)$ crossing count.  At $N{=}128$ (4 seeds), Clements collapses
to near-random accuracy ($26.3 \pm 2.9$\%) while butterfly retains $76.6 \pm 2.1$\%.
The optical depth of the mesh---not the MZI count---is the binding constraint
on PNN scalability.  All simulation code and data are open-source.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Photonic neural networks based on meshes of Mach--Zehnder interferometers implement
matrix--vector multiplication in the optical domain, offering potential advantages in
throughput and energy efficiency~\cite{shen2017,tsakyridis2024,bandyopadhyay2024}.
Multiple mesh topologies have been proposed: the Clements rectangular
mesh~\cite{clements2016}, the Reck triangular decomposition~\cite{reck1994},
butterfly/FFT architectures~\cite{tian2022,feng2022}, and sine-cosine fractal (SCF)
decompositions~\cite{basani2023}, among others~\cite{shokraneh2020,marchesin2025}.
Each proposal reports advantages using different metrics, noise models, and benchmarks,
making direct comparison difficult.

Three specific gaps persist in the literature.  First, no study incorporates
\emph{waveguide crossing loss} alongside per-MZI insertion loss when comparing
topologies.  Topologies such as butterfly and SCF fractal require waveguide crossings
that introduce additional optical loss; ignoring this mechanism biases comparisons in
their favor.  Second, existing comparisons are limited to small mesh sizes ($N \leq 32$)
and typically use at most two datasets.  Third, comparisons are pairwise and
incomplete: Fang~\textit{et al.}~\cite{fang2019} compare GridNet and FFTNet only;
Shokraneh~\textit{et al.}~\cite{shokraneh2020} evaluate Reck versus Diamond on
insertion-loss balancing without classification training;
Basani~\textit{et al.}~\cite{basani2023} compare SCF, Reck, and Clements on matrix
fidelity rather than end-to-end classification;
Shafiee~\textit{et al.}~\cite{shafiee2024} analyze loss and crosstalk for Clements,
Reck, and Diamond without training networks.
Gu~\textit{et al.}~\cite{gu2022} and Jiang~\textit{et al.}~\cite{jiang2025} perform
automated topology search within continuous supermesh parameterizations; our work
instead compares established named topologies head-to-head under identical conditions.

We address these gaps through four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A crossing-loss model applied in the differentiable forward pass, with crossing
        counts computed analytically for each topology and mesh size.
  \item Scaling from $N{=}4$ to $N{=}128$, revealing a ``depth wall'' where deep
        topologies collapse.
  \item Evaluation on four datasets (Vowel, MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10), providing
        broader evidence than prior two-dataset studies.
  \item An open-source simulation framework enabling reproduction and extension to
        additional topologies.
\end{enumerate}

All results reported in this paper are from simulation.  No experimental photonic
hardware was used.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{MZI physics model}
\label{sec:mzi}

Each MZI implements a $2 \times 2$ parameterized transformation:
\begin{equation}
U(\theta, \phi) = \begin{pmatrix}
  e^{i\phi}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\
  e^{i\phi}\sin\theta & \cos\theta
\end{pmatrix},
\label{eq:mzi}
\end{equation}
where $\theta$ is the internal phase shift and $\phi$ the external phase shift, both
learned during training via backpropagation through the optical mesh.

\textbf{Insertion loss.}
After each MZI, both output port amplitudes are attenuated by
$\alpha = \sqrt{10^{-L_{\mathrm{dB}}/10}}$, where $L_{\mathrm{dB}}$ is the per-MZI
insertion loss in dB.  Our default value is $L_{\mathrm{dB}} = 0.2$\,dB, consistent
with advanced silicon photonic foundries~\cite{shafiee2024}.  We note that
Clements~\textit{et al.}~\cite{clements2016} report 0.2\,dB per beam splitter; since
each MZI contains two beam splitters, our per-MZI figure represents an optimistic
target.  Results at 0.3 and 0.5\,dB/MZI bracket the more conservative estimate.
Signals traversing $d$ MZIs accumulate $d \times L_{\mathrm{dB}}$\,dB of loss, making
topology depth a first-order determinant of signal attenuation.

\textbf{Phase noise.}
Fabrication imprecision is modeled as additive Gaussian perturbations
$\theta \to \theta + \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$ and
$\phi \to \phi + \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$ applied independently to each MZI,
representing thermo-optic tuning errors typical of silicon photonic
foundries~\cite{bandyopadhyay2021}.  During evaluation, noise is applied at seven
levels ($\sigma = 0, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05, 0.1, 0.15, 0.2$\,rad) with 30 Monte Carlo
trials each.  The robustness ratio is defined as the mean accuracy at
$\sigma{=}0.2$\,rad divided by the clean accuracy ($\sigma{=}0$), representing the
fraction of performance retained under worst-case noise.
This single-point metric captures worst-case retention but not the full degradation
profile; the noise--accuracy curves in Figs.~\ref{fig:rob_fmnist}--\ref{fig:rob_vowel}
provide the complete picture.

\textbf{Photodetection.}
Square-law detection converts optical field amplitudes to intensities: $I_k = |E_k|^2$
at each output port $k$.  Classification is performed on the resulting intensity vector.


\subsection{Waveguide crossing loss model}
\label{sec:crossing}

Topologies that route non-adjacent waveguides through the same spatial region require
physical waveguide crossings.  Each crossing introduces additional optical loss beyond
MZI insertion loss.  We model crossing loss as a fixed attenuation per crossing event:
after a signal traverses a crossing, its amplitude is attenuated by
$\alpha_{\mathrm{x}} = \sqrt{10^{-L_{\mathrm{x}}/10}}$, where
$L_{\mathrm{x}}$ is the per-crossing loss in dB.

Our default value is $L_{\mathrm{x}} = 0.02$\,dB, consistent with measured values for
optimized silicon waveguide crossings~\cite{shafiee2024}.  We also evaluate
$L_{\mathrm{x}} = 0$ (no crossing loss), 0.05, and 0.10\,dB to assess sensitivity.

Crossing counts are computed analytically from each topology's wiring pattern.
Clements and Reck meshes use only adjacent-waveguide couplings and require zero
crossings.  Butterfly meshes require non-adjacent connections whose crossing count
grows as approximately $O(N^2)$.  SCF fractal meshes also require crossings,
also growing as approximately $O(N^2)$.  Table~\ref{tab:crossings} reports exact counts.


\subsection{Topology descriptions}
\label{sec:topos}

We evaluate four MZI mesh topologies, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:topologies} and
illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:schematics}.

\textbf{Clements}~\cite{clements2016}: a rectangular mesh of alternating even/odd
columns of adjacent-pair MZIs.  Universal, depth $N$, widely used in experimental
demonstrations~\cite{pai2023,shen2017}.  Requires zero waveguide crossings.

\textbf{Reck}~\cite{reck1994}: a triangular decomposition that nulls off-diagonal
matrix elements sequentially.  Universal, depth $2N{-}3$.  The triangular layout
produces highly unbalanced path lengths: some ports traverse nearly all MZIs while
corner ports traverse few.  Requires zero waveguide crossings.

\textbf{Butterfly/FFT}~\cite{tian2022,feng2022}: a butterfly network inspired by the
Cooley--Tukey FFT, with $\tfrac{N}{2}\log_2 N$ MZIs in $\log_2 N$ layers.  Not
universal---it implements a strict subset of $U(N)$---but the logarithmic depth
provides substantially lower cumulative insertion loss.  Requires waveguide crossings
that scale super-linearly with $N$.

\textbf{SCF Fractal}~\cite{basani2023}: a self-similar sine-cosine fractal
construction that recursively builds $N$-port unitaries from smaller blocks.
Universal, depth $N{-}1$, with a recursive structure that requires waveguide
crossings at each level of the fractal hierarchy.

\subsection{Network architecture}
\label{sec:arch}

The network consists of a single $N \times N$ MZI mesh implementing a unitary
(or sub-unitary, with losses) matrix--vector product, followed by square-law
photodetection and a learned electronic linear classifier.  Input data are encoded
as optical field amplitudes.  For datasets with dimensionality $d > N$, we apply
PCA to project inputs to $N$ dimensions before encoding (Table~\ref{tab:pca}).
For datasets with $d < N$, inputs are zero-padded.

Training uses the Adam optimizer with cross-entropy loss.  The MZI phase parameters
$(\theta, \phi)$ and the electronic classifier weights are trained jointly.
Insertion loss and crossing loss are applied in the forward pass during training,
so the network learns to compensate for these physical effects.  Phase noise is
\emph{not} applied during standard training; it is applied only during evaluation
(unless noise-aware training is explicitly indicated).

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}
\label{sec:eval}

Each configuration is trained for 100 epochs with 4 random seeds unless otherwise
noted.  Evaluation proceeds in two stages:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Clean accuracy}: the trained model is evaluated without phase noise
        to obtain the nominal accuracy.
  \item \textbf{Noise robustness}: phase noise at $\sigma \in \{0, 0.01, 0.02,
        0.05, 0.1, 0.15, 0.2\}$\,rad is injected, with 30 Monte Carlo trials per
        noise level.  The robustness ratio is defined as the mean accuracy at
        $\sigma{=}0.2$\,rad divided by the clean accuracy, representing the fraction
        of performance retained under the worst tested noise level.
\end{enumerate}

Hardware cost is defined as $\text{MZIs} \times \text{depth}$, providing a proxy
for the space--time product of the optical computation.

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

We evaluate on four classification tasks of varying difficulty:

\textbf{Vowel}~\cite{shen2017}: Deterding vowel recognition, 10 features, 11
classes, 528 training / 462 test samples.  A standard benchmark in the PNN
literature.

\textbf{MNIST}~\cite{lecun1998}: Handwritten digit classification, 784 features
(28$\times$28 images), 10 classes, 60\,000/10\,000 train/test split.

\textbf{Fashion-MNIST}~\cite{xiao2017}: Fashion article classification, same
dimensions as MNIST but more challenging, 10 classes.

\textbf{CIFAR-10}~\cite{krizhevsky2009}: Natural image classification, 3072
features (32$\times$32$\times$3), 10 classes, 50\,000/10\,000 train/test split.

Table~\ref{tab:pca} reports the fraction of input variance retained by PCA
projection to $N$ dimensions.

At $N{=}16$, accuracy differences among topologies are modest (within 2\,pp on
Vowel, within 1.4\,pp on MNIST), consistent with the observation that all four
topologies have sufficient expressivity for these tasks at small mesh size.
However, robustness differences are already substantial: butterfly achieves
$1.8\times$ the robustness of Clements on Vowel (0.377 vs.\ 0.208) and
$2.0\times$ on MNIST (0.342 vs.\ 0.169), a direct consequence of its
$\log_2 16 = 4$ depth versus Clements's depth of 16.

On the Pareto front of accuracy vs.\ hardware cost, butterfly dominates: it uses
only 32 MZIs (depth~4, cost $= 128$) compared to 120 MZIs (depth~16, cost $= 1920$)
for Clements, a $15\times$ cost reduction with comparable or superior accuracy.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Depth as the binding constraint}

The results consistently identify optical depth---not MZI count or
universality---as the primary determinant of PNN performance under realistic
fabrication conditions.  Each MZI layer contributes insertion loss that
attenuates the optical signal, and phase noise that accumulates across layers.
Butterfly's $O(\log N)$ depth provides a structural advantage that grows with
mesh size: at $N{=}16$ the depth ratio is $4{:}16$ (butterfly vs.\ Clements),
yielding a $1.8\times$ robustness advantage; at $N{=}64$ it is $6{:}64$, yielding
$2.1\times$; and at $N{=}128$, Clements fails entirely while butterfly is
unaffected.

The depth wall manifests as a sharp transition: Clements accuracy on Vowel is
73.9\% at $N{=}64$ but collapses to $26.3 \pm 2.9$\% at $N{=}128$, a ${\sim}48$\,pp
drop, while butterfly drops only from 78.0\% to $76.6 \pm 2.1$\% (within seed variance).

\subsection{The SCF fractal crossing-loss trap}

SCF fractal~\cite{basani2023} was proposed with favorable error-tolerance
properties derived from its self-similar structure.  In our simulations, it
performs competitively at $N{=}16$ (84.7\% on Vowel, the highest among all
topologies) but degrades sharply at $N{=}64$ (63.7\%, the lowest).  Two
factors contribute: (i)~its depth is ${\sim}N$, comparable to Clements, so it
suffers similar insertion-loss accumulation; and (ii)~its recursive structure
requires 12\,384 waveguide crossings at $N{=}64$, far more than butterfly's
5\,472.  The combination of deep MZI paths and high crossing counts makes SCF
fractal the worst performer at large $N$ in our simulations, despite its
universality and theoretical error-tolerance properties.

We caution that this result applies specifically to the fabrication-loss regime
we model.  In a hypothetical regime with negligible insertion and crossing loss,
SCF fractal's error-correction properties~\cite{basani2023} might confer
advantages not captured here.

\subsection{Reck's path-length distribution}

Reck achieves 81.5\% on Vowel at $N{=}64$, outperforming butterfly (78.0\%)
in absolute accuracy despite its $2N{-}3 = 125$ depth.  This is explained by
Reck's triangular structure: while some ports traverse up to 125 MZIs, many
ports traverse far fewer.  The mean path length is substantially less than the
maximum depth, and the highly asymmetric path-length distribution means that
some input channels experience much less loss than others.  The network can
learn to route information preferentially through the shorter paths.  However,
this comes at $219\times$ higher hardware cost (252\,000 vs.\ 1\,152) and lower
robustness (0.268 vs.\ 0.393).

\subsection{Crossing loss scaling}

The total crossings per port for butterfly is exactly $\frac{3}{2}(N - \log_2 N - 1)$,
which grows as ${\sim}\frac{3}{2}N$ for large $N$.  At 0.02\,dB per crossing, the
crossing-induced loss per port is $0.03(N - \log_2 N - 1)$\,dB: approximately 0.33\,dB
at $N{=}16$, 1.7\,dB at $N{=}64$, and 3.6\,dB at $N{=}128$.  At $N{=}128$, this
exceeds the MZI insertion loss ($7 \times 0.2 = 1.4$\,dB from the 7 MZI layers).
If crossing loss per intersection cannot be kept below 0.02\,dB, it will become the
dominant loss mechanism for butterfly at very large $N$.

\subsection{Fault-tolerant and reduced-depth architectures}

Hamerly~\textit{et al.}~\cite{hamerly2022nat} have proposed asymptotically
fault-tolerant MZI designs that could mitigate noise accumulation in deep meshes.
If such designs reduce per-MZI phase sensitivity, they would partially close the
depth gap, but not the insertion-loss gap (which is a physical, not
calibration-related, effect).
Tang~\textit{et al.}~\cite{tang2024} demonstrate a ten-port unitary processor
using reduced-depth architectures; such designs occupy a middle ground between
butterfly and the full-depth universal meshes.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Simulation only.}  All results are from numerical simulation with
        idealized noise models.  Real photonic hardware introduces additional effects
        (thermal crosstalk, wavelength-dependent loss, detector noise, beam-splitter
        ratio errors) not captured here.  Experimental validation is needed before
        drawing definitive conclusions.

  \item \textbf{Single optical layer.}  We evaluate a single MZI mesh followed by
        photodetection.  Multi-layer optical networks with optical nonlinearities
        between layers could change the topology tradeoffs.

  \item \textbf{Phase noise model.}  We use i.i.d.\ Gaussian phase noise, which
        may not capture correlated errors from thermal crosstalk or systematic
        calibration drift.

  \item \textbf{Crossing loss model.}  We model crossings as pure attenuation.
        Real crossings also introduce crosstalk between waveguides, which could
        have additional effects on classification performance~\cite{shafiee2024}.

  \item \textbf{PCA preprocessing.}  For high-dimensional datasets (MNIST,
        Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10), PCA reduces input dimensionality to $N$.
        This benefits all topologies equally but limits absolute accuracy and
        obscures potential differences in how topologies handle
        high-dimensional structure.  Eliminating PCA would require meshes of
        $N \geq 784$ (MNIST) or $N \geq 3072$ (CIFAR-10), which would further
        amplify the depth advantage---Clements at $N{=}784$ would accumulate
        $156.8$\,dB of insertion loss---but would also push butterfly's crossing
        loss into a potentially prohibitive regime (${\sim}23.5$\,dB per port at
        $N{=}784$ with 0.02\,dB/crossing), suggesting that large-scale PNN
        deployment may require topologies combining logarithmic depth with
        sub-quadratic crossing counts.

  \item \textbf{Limited scale.}  The largest mesh tested is $N{=}128$.
        Behavior at $N{=}256$ or larger, where crossing loss grows further,
        has not been evaluated.

  \item \textbf{Task scope.}  Classification on four standard datasets may
        not generalize to all PNN applications (e.g., signal processing,
        quantum state preparation).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Open-source framework}

The complete simulation framework---including topology generators, training code,
evaluation pipeline, and analysis scripts---is available as open source.  The
modular design allows adding new topologies by implementing a single mesh-generation
function.  We invite the community to extend the benchmark with additional
topologies, noise models, and experimental validation.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a simulation-based Pareto comparison of four MZI mesh topologies
for photonic neural networks, evaluated from $N{=}4$ to $N{=}128$ with per-MZI
insertion loss, waveguide crossing loss, and Monte Carlo phase noise on four
classification datasets.

The central finding is that optical depth, not MZI count or universality, is the
binding constraint on PNN scalability under realistic loss budgets.
Butterfly/FFT's $O(\log N)$ depth provides 3--5\,pp higher accuracy, up to $2.8\times$
better noise robustness, and $100\times$ lower hardware cost compared to Clements
at $N{=}64$.  At $N{=}128$, universal topologies collapse entirely while butterfly
retains useful accuracy.

Waveguide crossing loss, previously neglected in topology comparisons, is a
measurable but secondary effect at current technology levels (0.02\,dB/crossing):
it reduces butterfly accuracy by 0.7\,pp at $N{=}16$ and 3.2\,pp at $N{=}128$.
It is more problematic for SCF fractal, which combines deep paths with high
crossing counts.

These results suggest that PNN architectures designed for large-scale deployment
should prioritize depth reduction over universality.  The butterfly topology's
non-universality does not impair classification performance at any tested scale,
while its logarithmic depth provides structural resilience that cannot be matched
by training strategies applied to deep meshes.

All results are from simulation.  Experimental validation on fabricated photonic
chips is the essential next step.
